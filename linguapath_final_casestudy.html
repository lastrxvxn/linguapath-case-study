01 — Project Overview

What is LinguaPath?

LinguaPath is a hybrid EdTech platform built for adult professionals who understand English but lack the confidence to use it in high-stakes workplace situations — meetings, interviews, presentations, and cross-cultural collaboration.

The platform brings together three surfaces: a structured mobile app for daily practice, a web dashboard for learning planning and progress review, and a mentor support layer that provides the human feedback most digital language tools skip entirely.

Unlike consumer language apps optimised for streaks and casual engagement, LinguaPath treats its users as goal-driven adults. Every design decision — from the learning roadmap to the mentor booking flow — was made to serve one outcome: helping professionals communicate with confidence in real work situations.

The three gaps this product addresses

  • The absence of structured career-focused content in mainstream language apps
  • The lack of visible, multi-dimensional progress tracking
  • The missing human feedback layer that builds real communicative confidence

The three-surface approach

  • Mobile: daily practice sessions, short and focused
  • Web dashboard: progress review and learning planning
  • Mentor layer: guided feedback at key progression checkpoints

02 — The Problem

The Intermediate Plateau

Millions of professionals have reached a functional level of English. They can follow conversations, read documents, and understand most of what they hear. But when it's their turn to speak in a meeting, write a difficult email, or present to an international audience — they freeze.

This is known in language acquisition research as the intermediate plateau: a stage where passive comprehension exists but active production lags far behind. The learner has enough English to get by, but not enough confidence to perform under pressure.

"I understand everything in the meetings, but when it's my turn to speak, I go blank. I sound competent in Portuguese — not in English."

Where existing platforms fall short

Most language apps are built around engagement mechanics — streaks, points, short exercises, and gamified reward loops. These are effective at creating habits, but they address the wrong problem for this audience. Adult professionals are not struggling with motivation. They are struggling with structure, context, and confidence.

What existing apps do well

  • Vocabulary building and grammar reinforcement
  • Daily habit formation through streaks
  • Accessible and low-friction entry for beginners

Where they fall short

  • Lessons lack professional workplace context
  • No structured progression for intermediate learners
  • Progress measured in streaks, not skill growth
  • No human feedback layer for communication practice

03 — Target Users

Who this product is for

LinguaPath is designed for adult professionals at the intermediate level — people who have studied English before and can function in English, but who need to perform in it consistently and confidently at work.

Remote workers

Collaborating daily with international colleagues via async tools, video calls, and written communication

Career switchers

Moving into global companies or international roles where English is the primary working language

Interview candidates

Preparing for English-language hiring processes at international or multinational organisations

Intermediate learners

Stuck at a plateau — they understand English well but freeze when active communication is required

04 — Research Insights

What the research revealed

Research combined direct observation from ten years of teaching professional English learners, competitive analysis of major language learning platforms, and review of adult learning literature. Five consistent patterns shaped every design decision.

Research inputs: observation of adult learner patterns · platform competitive analysis · professional teaching experience · review of challenges in international workplace transitions

#InsightDesign implication
1 Adult learners are goal-driven Every screen connects directly to a declared career goal. The learning path is generated from a specific outcome, not an open topic library.
2 Visible progress sustains motivation Progress must be multi-dimensional. A single completion percentage is not enough — learners need to see improvement across specific communication skills over time.
3 Real-world context increases engagement Lessons must simulate professional scenarios — meetings, status updates, emails, presentations. Generic vocabulary drills are disengaging for this audience.
4 Structure reduces abandonment Adult learners stay engaged longer when the experience has a clear roadmap and defined endpoint. Open content libraries increase drop-off at the intermediate stage.
5 Mentor feedback builds real confidence The gap between passive understanding and active communication cannot be closed through practice alone. Human feedback from a qualified guide is a critical accelerator.

07 — Product Strategy

Core product principles

Instead of designing a traditional language learning app focused on vocabulary and repetition, the goal was to create a structured professional development tool. Each decision — from the learning roadmap to the mentor layer — reinforces clarity, progress visibility, and real-world communication practice.

01

Structure over abundance

A defined learning path rather than an overwhelming library of lessons. Learners always know what comes next.

02

Context over coverage

Lessons built around real workplace situations: meetings, emails, interviews, and presentations.

03

Feedback as a product feature

Mentor support integrated into the learning journey as a core differentiator, not an optional extra.

04

Progress visibility

The dashboard shows learners how their specific communication skills are evolving over time.

08 — Information Architecture

Mobile App

Daily learning experience. Short, focused sessions for commutes and lunch breaks. All core lesson content and the primary learning loop.

Web Dashboard

Progress tracking and learning planning. Learners review skill growth, view their roadmap, and access mentor session history on a larger canvas.

Human feedback and guided review. Matched by specialisation. Woven into the learning path at key milestone checkpoints — not an optional add-on.

10 — Key Screens

Product screens — MVP

8 core screens across mobile and web. Each screen is designed around a single primary action, a clear information hierarchy, and a direct connection to the learner's declared career goal.

Mobile · Onboarding · Step 1

01 — Goal Selection

Establishes the learner's contract with the product. The goal selected here determines module content, lesson types, vocabulary register, and mentor specialisation matching. Must feel like a professional intake — not a quiz.

Core components

  • Step indicator (3 dots, active/inactive)
  • Headline + sub-headline
  • 4 tappable goal cards (icon + label + description)
  • Secondary link: 'I have more than one goal'
  • Primary CTA: 'Continue' — disabled until selection

UX rationale

Single-question forced-choice reduces cognitive load and increases completion. Disabling the CTA prevents skipping the step that determines the entire learning path. The professional framing signals from the first screen that this is not a casual app.

Mobile · Onboarding · 5 questions

02 — Skill Assessment

Establishes a communication baseline using situational workplace prompts rather than grammar exercises. This signals from the very first question that the product is about real communication, not textbook English.

Core components

  • Progress bar (question X of 5)
  • Scenario context card (teal fill, overline label)
  • Question prompt (16px, medium weight)
  • 4 answer rows with radio indicators
  • Hint: 'No wrong answers — this calibrates your path'

UX rationale

Auto-advance on selection removes a tap and keeps momentum. The 'no wrong answers' framing reduces evaluation anxiety — a common barrier for intermediate learners already self-conscious about their English ability.

Mobile · Post-onboarding reveal

03 — Personalised Learning Roadmap

The payoff screen. The user sees their complete learning path for the first time — structured progression from current level to declared goal. Must feel personal and achievable simultaneously.

Core components

  • Header: user name + goal + duration badge (~8 weeks)
  • Overall progress bar (4px, labeled)
  • Vertical milestone timeline with connecting spine
  • 5 module cards: active / upcoming / locked states
  • Module 5: purple mentor milestone indicator
  • Sticky CTA: 'Start Module 1'

UX rationale

The roadmap solves the most common driver of abandonment: 'how long will this take?' going unanswered. Showing the complete path with a time estimate gives learners an answer before they ask. Locked-state progression makes advancement feel earned, not punitive.

Mobile · Recurring · 4-tab navigation

04 — Daily Learning Home

The surface the user returns to every day. Must answer three questions instantly: Where am I? What should I do today? How am I doing? It is a launching pad, not a content library.

Core components

  • Header: greeting with name + avatar circle
  • Module progress bar + sub-label
  • Featured 'Today's lesson' card (teal 1.5px border)
  • Meta: duration icon + lesson type label
  • Primary CTA: 'Continue lesson'
  • Weekly activity strip (7 day dots)
  • Mentor reminder card (conditional)
  • Tab bar: Home / Learn / Mentor / Progress

UX rationale

A single dominant CTA removes choice paralysis for returning users. The weekly activity strip communicates practice consistency without the anxiety mechanics of a streak counter that 'breaks' when a day is missed.

Mobile · Focused task flow · Tab bar hidden

05 — Lesson Screen

The core product experience. Every design decision supports one goal: practising real professional communication in a realistic workplace context, without distraction.

Core components

  • Exit button (left) + step counter (right)
  • Lesson progress bar (step-level, not module)
  • Lesson type chip (written / spoken / scenario choice)
  • Scenario context card (teal-50, 'Workplace scenario')
  • Task prompt (15px, medium weight)
  • Response area: textarea / choices / mic button
  • 'Show tip' ghost button + 'Submit' primary button

UX rationale

The scenario card grounds every task in professional reality. Hiding the tip by default preserves productive cognitive effort while ensuring no learner feels stranded. Removing points and timers keeps focus on communication quality, not speed.

Mobile · Mentor tab

06 — Mentor Booking

Where the human layer becomes tangible. The design communicates trust, specificity, and ease. The learner has just identified a skill gap — booking should feel like a natural next step.

Core components

  • Page header: 'Book a session' + '60-minute guided review'
  • Mentor profile card: avatar, name, spec tag, bio, stats
  • Session type selector: 3 radio rows
  • 6-column weekly availability grid
  • Dynamic CTA: 'Confirm — Fri 6:00 PM'

UX rationale

Showing the mentor's specialisation before the calendar confirms this is a matched professional. The session type selector connects the booking directly to the learner's current skill gap, making the session feel purposeful before it starts.

Web · Sidebar navigation

07 — Progress Overview Dashboard

The central web view. Translates all learning activity into a clear picture of communication skill growth. A learner should understand their status within 30 seconds of arriving.

Core components

  • Fixed sidebar: brand, navigation (5 items), sign out
  • Page greeting + module context sub-label
  • 3-column metric strip: lessons / module / days active
  • Skill radar chart (5 axes)
  • 2-col: radar (left) + activity feed (right)
  • Full-width recommended action strip

UX rationale

The radar chart is the most important design decision. It replaces a single progress percentage with a multi-dimensional view of communication skill. A learner who scores well on Writing but low on Speaking sees that asymmetry immediately — and knows exactly what to focus on.

Web · Progress drill-down

08 — Skills Progress Detail

The deep-dive view. When a learner selects a skill dimension, they see how it has changed over 6 weeks, which sessions contributed, and what their mentor said. This screen makes the product's premium value most visible.

Core components

  • 5-tab skill selector (one per dimension)
  • Score display: large number + change indicator
  • 6-week progress line chart (full-width)
  • Contributing sessions table (left column)
  • Mentor feedback excerpt with left-border quote block
  • Recommended next action strip

UX rationale

The mentor feedback excerpt is the most powerful element on this screen. Seeing a professional's specific observation — 'she tends to trail off before finishing key points' — is what converts a sceptical learner into a committed one. It is also the clearest signal that LinguaPath offers something no automated app can replicate.

Typography

Recommended typeface: DM Sans. A humanist geometric sans-serif with excellent legibility at small sizes and a warm, professional quality at display sizes. The 300 weight for display headings creates openness; the 500 weight for UI labels communicates structure without heaviness.

12 — Expected Outcomes

Expected outcomes

The following outcomes are projections based on the product design decisions and comparable EdTech benchmarks. Clearly labelled as expected — no fabricated metrics are used.

Clearer learning progression

A user completing onboarding as designed would reach their first lesson within 8 minutes of sign-up, with a specific goal selected and a clear 8-week roadmap generated. This reduces the 'I don't know where to start' drop-off that drives early abandonment in language apps.

Improved learner confidence in professional communication

A learner using the structured roadmap for 4 weeks would complete 2–3 modules, have at least one mentor session, and be able to name specific improvement areas from their skill profile — rather than the vague 'I've been practising' feeling most apps produce.

Stronger transfer to workplace communication

Because every lesson is framed by a professional scenario before the task, learners would be expected to transfer practice skills more readily to real workplace situations compared to context-free vocabulary drills.

Visible, multi-dimensional skill growth

The radar chart translates learning activity into a view of communication ability that maps to how language actually works. Learners who can see their Writing score improving while Speaking needs work engage more deliberately — and feel a greater sense of progress.

Increased engagement through real-world scenarios and mentor support

Retention for motivated adult professionals would be expected to outperform casual gamified apps because the product is designed around their actual goal — career advancement — rather than engagement loops and daily streaks.

13 — Reflection

Reflection

My ten years as an English instructor gave me something most UX designers working in EdTech don't have: I know what it actually feels like to be in the room with a struggling intermediate learner.

What teaching gave this design

I have watched professionals who speak three languages freeze the moment they switch to English in front of an international audience. I have seen learners who can pass a grammar test stumble through a job interview. For ten years, I designed learning materials that had to work in the real world — not just look good in a presentation.

This background shaped every design decision in LinguaPath. The problem statement came from something I observed directly. The personas reflect composite portraits of real learners. The scenario-based lesson structure comes from a pedagogical approach I used in adult classrooms — situational learning is significantly more effective for professional communication than decontextualised grammar drills. The mentor layer exists because I know from experience that feedback from a qualified person is irreplaceable.

What UX design added

Transitioning into UX showed me that learning experience design and product design share more than they appear to. Both require deep empathy. Both require clear information architecture — knowing what comes first, what comes next, and why. Both require making complex things feel simple without removing the substance.

The difference is that UX design makes those decisions visible, testable, and iteratable. A curriculum designer says 'I think this lesson sequence works.' A UX designer can define the hypothesis, design the flow, test it, measure abandonment, and revise. That loop is more honest and more powerful.

What this project represents

LinguaPath is a design response to a real problem I have spent a decade watching go unsolved — the gap between passive English competence and confident professional communication. The research insights are real. The personas are grounded in actual learners. The design decisions are rooted in what I know about how adults learn under pressure.

That is the contribution I bring to any product team working in EdTech: a designer who does not just research the user — but who has been in the room with them for ten years.
